DIN vs ISO vs ASTM: Fastener Standards Explained for International Buyers

Technical Guide 21 June 2026 6 min read Standards & Specifications

  3. ISO — International Standards
  4. ASTM — American Standards

When you order fasteners internationally, a simple size ("M12 × 80 hex bolt") is not a specification. The same size exists under DIN 931, ISO 4014, ASTM F568, and half a dozen other standards — with subtly different tolerances, material requirements, and property class designations. Getting this wrong means receiving product that technically fulfils your purchase order but doesn't comply with your project's engineering requirements.

This guide explains what each major standard system covers, where it is used, how they relate to each other, and which to specify when sourcing from India.

Why Standards Matter in Fastener Procurement

A fastener standard defines three things: dimensions (thread form, pitch, head size, length tolerances), mechanical properties (tensile strength, yield strength, hardness), and material requirements (chemical composition, heat treatment). Without a specified standard, the supplier makes assumptions — and those assumptions may not match your engineer's intent.

Standards also define what a Mill Test Certificate (MTC) must contain and what tests must be performed. A fastener certified to ISO 898-1 Class 8.8 has been tested to specific tensile, yield, and hardness requirements and the MTC must record the results. An uncertified "8.8" bolt may or may not meet those requirements.

DIN — Deutsches Institut für Normung (German Standards)

DIN fastener standards were developed by Germany's national standards body and became the de facto European metric fastener specification from the 1950s through the 1990s. DIN numbers are still widely used in specifications, purchase orders, and technical drawings across Europe, the Middle East, and parts of Asia — particularly in older infrastructure, legacy projects, and countries that adopted German industrial practice early.

DIN standards you will commonly encounter:

  • DIN 931 — Hex bolt, partially threaded (now largely superseded by ISO 4014)
  • DIN 933 — Hex bolt, fully threaded (ISO 4017 equivalent)
  • DIN 934 — Hex nut (ISO 4032 equivalent)
  • DIN 125A — Plain washer (ISO 7089 equivalent)
  • DIN 127B — Spring lock washer
  • DIN 975 / 976 — Threaded rod (metre length / cut length)
  • DIN 529 — Foundation bolt (J-bolt)
  • DIN 7990 — Structural bolt assembly (superseded by EN 14399)

DIN standards are published in German; property classes reference ISO 898-1 (4.6, 8.8, etc.). Many DIN standards have been officially withdrawn and replaced by their ISO equivalents, but the DIN numbers remain in common use.

ISO — International Organization for Standardization

ISO fastener standards are internationally agreed specifications developed by a committee of national standards bodies. They are the current best practice and are adopted (with national labels) by most countries: BS EN ISO in the UK, DIN EN ISO in Germany, NF EN ISO in France, etc. For metric fasteners, ISO standards are the most precise and internationally unambiguous specification you can use.

Key ISO fastener standards:

  • ISO 4014 — Hex bolt, partially threaded, grades 5.6–10.9
  • ISO 4017 — Hex bolt, fully threaded
  • ISO 4032 — Hex nut, style 1
  • ISO 4033 — Hex nut, style 2 (higher)
  • ISO 7089 — Plain washer, normal series
  • ISO 7093 — Plain washer, large series
  • ISO 898-1 — Mechanical properties of bolts (property classes)
  • ISO 898-2 — Mechanical properties of nuts
  • ISO 1461 — Hot-dip galvanizing specification
  • EN 14399 — High-strength structural bolt assemblies for preloading

ASTM — American Society for Testing and Materials

ASTM standards are the dominant specification in the USA and are widely used in the Middle East oil and gas sector, parts of Latin America, and some Southeast Asian markets that follow US engineering codes. ASTM uses a fundamentally different system from DIN/ISO: it specifies fasteners by application grade (A307, A325, A490, etc.) rather than property class number, and primarily addresses inch-series (imperial) fasteners, though metric ASTM standards exist.

Key ASTM fastener standards:

  • ASTM A307 Grade A — Low-strength hex bolts (inch), ~414 MPa tensile, general purpose
  • ASTM A325 — High-strength structural bolts (inch), 830 MPa tensile, ~ISO 8.8 equivalent
  • ASTM A490 — High-strength structural bolts (inch), 1040 MPa tensile, ~ISO 10.9 equivalent
  • ASTM A193 Grade B7 — Alloy steel stud bolts and headed bolts for high-temp/pressure flanges
  • ASTM A194 Grade 2H — Nuts for high-pressure service, pairs with A193 B7
  • ASTM F1554 — Anchor bolts, grades 36/55/105
  • ASTM F593 / F594 — Stainless steel bolts and nuts, inch series
  • ASTM F568 — Metric carbon and alloy steel bolts (specifies ISO thread and ISO property classes)

BS, JIS, and BIS

Other standards you may encounter:

  • BS (British Standards): BS 3692, BS 4190 for hex bolts; BS 4882 for stud bolts. Post-Brexit, the UK is transitioning these to BS ISO. Mostly interchangeable with ISO for common types.
  • JIS (Japanese Industrial Standards): Used in Japan and some Southeast Asian markets. JIS B 1180 (hex bolts) is dimensionally similar to ISO but with different mechanical property designations.
  • BIS (Bureau of Indian Standards): IS 1364 covers hex bolts and nuts; IS 5624 covers foundation bolts. BIS standards generally align with ISO dimensions and ISO 898-1 mechanical requirements. Used for domestic Indian projects; international buyers almost always specify DIN/ISO/ASTM.

Which Standard to Specify When Ordering from India

For Europe, Middle East, Africa, and most of Asia: Specify ISO standards with property class. This is unambiguous, internationally tested, and what Indian manufacturers certify against. Example: "Hex Bolt ISO 4014, Class 8.8, M16 × 2.0 × 80mm, Hot-Dip Galvanized ISO 1461."

For legacy DIN specifications: DIN numbers are accepted — just add the ISO property class for clarity since DIN and ISO are equivalent for most types.

For USA / ASTM projects: Specify ASTM explicitly — A307, A325, A193 B7, F1554, etc. Confirm the supplier's ASTM test capability and ensure the MTC references the ASTM standard and grade, not just ISO property class.

For UK post-Brexit: BS EN ISO or BS ISO standards are fine. The dimensions and properties are identical to ISO.
