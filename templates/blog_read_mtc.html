How to Read a Mill Test Certificate (MTC): A Buyer's Guide

You receive a pallet of bolts from India. The invoice says "Grade 8.8 per ISO 898-1." The box is marked. But how do you know the material is actually what was claimed? The answer is the Mill Test Certificate (MTC) — and knowing how to read one is one of the most practical skills an import buyer can have. This article explains exactly what to look for.

What Is a Mill Test Certificate?

A Mill Test Certificate (also called a Material Test Report, or MTR) is a traceability document issued at the time of production that records the actual measured properties of a specific batch of material. It ties test results to a heat number — a unique identifier for the batch of steel from which the material was made. This chain from test result → heat number → fastener lot is what makes a claim of conformance verifiable rather than assumed.

MTCs are governed by EN 10204:2004 in Europe (and widely referenced globally, including in the Gulf, Southeast Asia, and Australia). EN 10204 defines four types of document, each offering a different level of assurance.

EN 10204 Types Explained

Type Name Based On Signed By Typical Use
2.1 Declaration of Compliance Non-specific inspection; product standard Manufacturer Low-risk, commercial-grade bulk supply
2.2 Test Report Non-specific inspection; may use typical values Manufacturer General commercial; some tender requirements
3.1 Inspection Certificate 3.1 Specific inspection of the batch supplied Manufacturer's authorised representative (independent of production) Standard for structural, oil & gas, construction, industrial imports
3.2 Inspection Certificate 3.2 Specific inspection of the batch supplied Manufacturer's representative + independent third party or customer representative Nuclear, aerospace, safety-critical, customer-witnessed testing

TERNS EXIM provides EN 10204 Type 3.1 MTCs on every order as standard. Type 2.2 is available where a buyer's specification explicitly permits it; 3.2 with third-party witness (SGS, Bureau Veritas, or TÜV) is available on request for critical orders.

Never accept a Type 2.1 document for structural fasteners. It contains no actual test data — it is simply a manufacturer's statement that the product conforms to a standard, without any test evidence attached.

Reading the Heat Number

The heat number (also called melt number, cast number, or charge number) is the most important field on the MTC. It identifies the specific batch of molten steel from which the material was cast. Every other field on the certificate relates to testing performed on material drawn from that heat.

When you receive a shipment, verify that:

  • The heat number on the MTC matches the heat number on the package markings or delivery note
  • The quantity on the MTC is consistent with (or covers) the quantity shipped
  • The product description on the MTC matches what you ordered (size, standard, grade)

A mismatched heat number is a red flag — it may indicate the certificate has been re-used from a different batch. Request clarification before accepting the goods.

Chemical Composition Section

The chemical composition table shows the measured percentage of each element in the steel. For carbon and alloy steel fasteners (grades 4.6 through 12.9 per ISO 898-1), the key elements are:

  • Carbon (C) — governs hardness and strength. ISO 898-1 limits C to ≤ 0.55% for grades through 12.9. Higher carbon increases strength but reduces weldability and toughness.
  • Manganese (Mn) — hardenability element; typical range 0.50–1.10% for structural grades.
  • Phosphorus (P) and Sulphur (S) — impurities; ISO 898-1 limits P ≤ 0.048% and S ≤ 0.058%. Elevated levels indicate lower-quality steel and increase brittleness.
  • Boron (B) — micro-alloy hardenability additive. ISO 898-1 Annex A restricts boron content for certain grades and sizes due to hydrogen embrittlement risk.
  • Chromium (Cr) and Molybdenum (Mo) — alloy steel elements used in higher grades (10.9 and 12.9) for deep hardenability.

Compare each value against the limits in the applicable standard (ISO 898-1, ASTM A307, or whichever was specified). If any element exceeds the standard's limit, the material is non-conforming regardless of what the mechanical test results show.

Mechanical Properties Section

This section records the results of destructive testing on specimens drawn from the heat. For bolts and fasteners, the key values are:

  • Proof load / Proof stress (MPa) — the load at which 0.0048D permanent elongation occurs (ISO 898-1 proof load test). For grade 8.8 M12: 580 MPa.
  • Tensile strength / Ultimate tensile strength, UTS (MPa) — the maximum load before fracture. Must meet the ISO minimum for the stated grade (800 MPa for 8.8, 1040 MPa for 10.9, 1220 MPa for 12.9).
  • Yield strength / 0.2% proof stress (MPa) — the stress at which 0.2% permanent strain occurs. Must meet grade minimum (e.g. 640 MPa for 8.8).
  • Elongation at break (%) — ductility indicator. ISO 898-1 requires ≥12% for grade 8.8, ≥9% for 10.9, ≥8% for 12.9.

A test result that meets tensile strength but fails elongation indicates brittle material — this is as serious a non-conformance as a strength shortfall.

Hardness Results

Hardness testing (Vickers HV or Rockwell HRC) provides a rapid non-destructive cross-check of material condition. ISO 898-1 specifies hardness ranges for each grade:

  • Grade 8.8: 245–320 HV (23–34 HRC)
  • Grade 10.9: 320–380 HV (33–39 HRC)
  • Grade 12.9: 385–435 HV (39–45 HRC)

A result below the minimum indicates under-hardening (insufficient quench or too-low carbon). A result above the maximum is often more dangerous — over-hardened material is brittle and susceptible to stress-corrosion cracking. Both are grounds for rejection.

Why It Matters for Import QA and Customs

An MTC is increasingly required by importers' own QA systems and, in some jurisdictions, by customs or certification bodies. In the EU, Construction Products Regulation (CPR) requires traceability documentation for structural fasteners used in CE-marked assemblies. In the UAE and Saudi Arabia, SASO and ESMA standards require MTCs for many categories of imported fasteners.

Beyond compliance, the practical risk of operating without MTCs is real: multiple product liability cases in construction and manufacturing have turned on whether the buyer had documentation proving the material met specification at time of supply. An MTC is that documentation.

What to Verify Before Accepting a Shipment

Run through this checklist when an MTC arrives with a shipment:

  • Heat number matches the delivery note and package markings
  • Document type is EN 10204 3.1 or higher (not 2.1 or 2.2 unless your spec permits)
  • Product description, standard, and grade match your purchase order
  • Chemical composition values are within standard limits — especially P and S
  • Tensile strength, yield strength, and elongation all meet grade minimums
  • Hardness result is within the ISO 898-1 range for the stated grade
  • Certificate is signed by the authorised signatory (not just stamped)
  • Date of test is consistent with the production batch — not backdated years prior
