How to Verify an Indian Fastener Exporter: A Buyer's Due-Diligence Guide

India's fastener export market spans thousands of manufacturers and trading companies, ranging from large ISO-certified factories to small intermediaries with no direct manufacturing capability. The price difference between a compliant, certified supplier and an uncertified one can appear minimal on paper — but the quality, documentation, and reliability difference can be enormous. A failed fastener in a structural application, or a shipment rejected at customs for missing documentation, costs far more than the saving on unit price.

This guide gives you a systematic framework to verify an Indian fastener supplier before committing to a commercial order.

Why Verification Matters

The risks of skipping due diligence on an Indian fastener supplier include:

  • Sub-grade material: Bolts marked "8.8" but made from incorrect material with tensile strength significantly below 800 MPa
  • Missing or forged MTCs: Test certificates that reference real tests but were not actually performed on the supplied batch
  • Intermediary passing off: A trader presenting themselves as a manufacturer, with no control over the actual production quality
  • Customs delays: Incorrect HS codes, missing Certificate of Origin, or non-compliant invoices causing clearance problems at your port
  • Payment loss: Advance payment to a non-delivering supplier with no recourse

None of these risks can be fully eliminated, but a structured pre-order verification process reduces them substantially.

Step 1: Verify Legal Registration

Import Export Code (IEC): Every legal Indian exporter holds an IEC — a 10-digit number issued by the DGFT (Directorate General of Foreign Trade). Ask for the IEC number and verify it on the DGFT portal. The portal returns the company name, registered address, and active/inactive status. A mismatch between the IEC company name and the name on the quotation/invoice is a red flag.

GST Registration: Indian businesses with turnover above ₹20 lakh (approx. USD 24,000) are required to register for GST (Goods and Services Tax). All export invoices from GST-registered exporters are zero-rated (0% GST on exports), but the 15-digit GSTIN must appear on the invoice. An exporter without a GSTIN is operating below the registration threshold — which for an international fastener exporter is implausible — or is unregistered.

Company incorporation: You can verify company registration (PAN, incorporation date, directors) on the Ministry of Corporate Affairs portal (mca.gov.in) for Private Limited or Limited companies. Sole proprietors and partnerships have lighter registration requirements, but should still hold IEC and GST.

Step 2: Check Quality Certifications

ISO 9001:2015 quality management system certification is the baseline credibility marker for a manufacturing supplier. Request the current certificate — not just the logo on the website — and verify the certificate number with the issuing body (DNV, Bureau Veritas, TÜV, etc.) on their registry. Certificates expire and must be renewed; a lapsed certificate provides no assurance.

For stainless steel fasteners, look for raw material mill certifications (e.g., from SAIL, Bhushan Steel, or imported coil with EN 10204 3.1 MTC) to confirm the wire rod source.

Note that ISO 9001 certifies the quality management process, not product quality directly. A supplier can be ISO 9001-certified and still supply non-conforming product. ISO 9001 matters as a positive indicator, not as a guarantee.

Step 3: Assess Mill Test Certificate Capability

Ask the supplier to provide a sample MTC from a recent shipment before you place your order. A credible MTC should contain:

  • Heat/melt number (traceable to the raw material batch)
  • Chemical composition results with standard specification limits
  • Tensile strength, yield strength, and elongation test results
  • Hardness test results (Vickers HV or Rockwell)
  • Applicable standard and property class reference (e.g., ISO 898-1, Class 8.8)
  • Test laboratory name, date, and signatory

A supplier who cannot produce a sample MTC, or who produces a document that is clearly a template without actual test values, is not providing genuine material testing. Walk away.

For critical applications, specify EN 10204 Type 3.1 (test witnessed by an independent inspector or accredited internal laboratory) rather than Type 2.2 (manufacturer self-declaration).

Step 4: Request a Sample Order

Always request a sample shipment before your first commercial order. A sample of 25–100 pieces of your specified fastener allows you to:

  • Verify thread dimensions with a calibrated gauge (pitch, major diameter, minor diameter)
  • Check head dimensions against the DIN/ISO/ASTM drawing
  • Verify coating thickness (if specified) with a coating thickness gauge
  • Perform a hardness check (Rockwell or Vickers) against the property class limits
  • Check that head markings (property class + manufacturer ID) are present and legible

Legitimate suppliers will supply samples routinely — often at cost or free for genuine commercial enquiries. A supplier who insists on a large minimum order with no sample option should be treated with caution.

Step 5: Third-Party Inspection Readiness

For commercial orders above USD 10,000, or for safety-critical applications, arrange pre-shipment third-party inspection by SGS, Bureau Veritas (BV), or TÜV. The inspector visits the factory or warehouse before loading, verifies the goods against the purchase order specification, confirms the MTC values, performs spot hardness and dimensional checks, and issues an inspection certificate.

A supplier who welcomes third-party inspection is confident in their product. A supplier who resists, delays, or inflates costs around inspection is a warning sign. Inspection adds approximately 0.3–0.8% to the order value and is almost always worth it for first orders with a new supplier.

Step 6: Payment Security

For first orders with a new supplier, use a Letter of Credit (L/C). An L/C is a bank-to-bank instrument where your bank guarantees payment only when the supplier presents a conforming set of shipping documents. This provides strong protection: the supplier cannot receive payment without actually shipping correctly documented goods.

Alternatively, some buyers use escrow services or split the payment: a small advance (20–30%) to confirm the order, with the balance held until the third-party inspection certificate is issued. Avoid paying 100% advance to an unverified new supplier for a large order — your recourse in a dispute will be limited to expensive international arbitration.

Step 7: References and Track Record

Ask for two or three references — buyers in your country or a comparable market who have received multiple shipments. A supplier with 5+ years of exports to the USA, UAE, UK, or Europe will readily provide verifiable contacts. Call or email the references and ask specifically about: delivery timeliness, quality of MTC documentation, responsiveness to problems, and whether they would reorder.

Also check if the supplier appears in any export databases (Zauba, Volza, ImportYeti) — these show actual shipping records from Indian customs data and can confirm that the supplier has genuinely exported the products they claim to.

Red Flags to Watch For

  • Cannot provide IEC number or it does not verify on DGFT portal
  • Refuses to supply samples before a commercial order
  • Resists third-party inspection or creates obstacles around it
  • MTC is clearly a template with no actual test values or heat numbers
  • Prices significantly below market (20–30% under prevailing export prices for the same specification)
  • No verifiable export history — not in any shipping database for the products they claim to supply
  • Requests 100% advance payment via informal channels (personal bank accounts, cryptocurrency, informal transfer services)
  • Cannot answer detailed technical questions about manufacturing process, material sourcing, or coating process
  • Website or documents contain images that reverse-image-search to different companies
