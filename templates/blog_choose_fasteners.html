How to Choose the Right Fastener: Size, Grade & Material Guide

How-To Guide 23 June 2026 8 min read Fastener Selection
In this article:
  1. Step 1 — Determine the right size (diameter & length)
  2. Step 2 — Choose coarse or fine thread pitch
  3. Step 3 — Select the grade by load and joint type
  4. Step 4 — Choose material by environment
  5. Step 5 — Select the finish by corrosion exposure
  6. Fastener selection checklist

Ordering the wrong fastener costs more than just the price of a replacement box. A bolt that is under-strength fails at the joint. A bolt that is over-specified raises cost, weight, and sometimes creates brittleness risks. This guide walks through the five decisions — size, thread pitch, grade, material, and finish — in the order you should make them.

Step 1 — Determine the Right Size: Diameter and Length

The two key dimensions are the nominal diameter (the thread's major diameter, e.g. M12 = 12 mm) and the grip length (the thickness of the material being clamped).

Nominal diameter

Diameter is determined by the load the joint must carry, divided across the number of bolts. For structural steel, this calculation follows EN 1993-1-8 or AISC 360 depending on your standard. For general machinery, a practical rule of thumb is that the bolt diameter should be approximately equal to the plate thickness — an M12 bolt through a 12 mm plate is a reasonable starting point. Always verify through a load calculation for any safety-critical application.

Grip length and bolt length

The grip length is the total thickness of all plies being clamped. The bolt length should be long enough that at least one full thread protrudes beyond the nut after tightening (one pitch beyond the nut face is the ISO minimum), but not so long that excessive thread engages inside the joint shear plane. Standard bolt length series under ISO 4014 (hex bolt, partial thread) and ISO 4017 (hex bolt, full thread) are published in 5 mm and 10 mm increments for most sizes — select the shortest bolt that provides adequate thread protrusion.

Thread engagement: the nut must engage at least one nominal diameter's worth of threads for full proof load. A M16 bolt requires at least 16 mm of nut thread engagement — a standard M16 hex nut (ISO 4032) provides this.

Step 2 — Choose Coarse or Fine Thread Pitch

ISO metric threads are defined by diameter and pitch (e.g. M16 × 2 for coarse, M16 × 1.5 for fine). Coarse pitch is the default and is used in the vast majority of structural, construction, and general industrial applications. Fine pitch is reserved for specific engineering situations:

  • Coarse pitch (ISO standard pitch) — faster assembly, more damage-tolerant, widely stocked, all standard nuts fit. Use for 95% of applications.
  • Fine pitch — higher thread-stripping resistance per unit length, useful in thin-walled tapped holes, precision adjustment mechanisms, or where fine torque control is critical. Requires matching fine-pitch nuts — never mix with coarse-pitch hardware.

Step 3 — Select the Grade by Load and Joint Type

Property class (grade) sets the bolt's mechanical strength. Three grades cover the majority of industrial applications — refer to our detailed article on bolt grades 8.8 vs 10.9 vs 12.9 for the full breakdown. A quick decision guide:

Grade Min. Tensile Best For Avoid When
4.6 400 MPa Light non-structural, access panels, formwork Any structural or high-load joint
8.8 800 MPa Structural steel, general machinery, hot-dip galvanized joints Preloaded HSFG connections (use 10.9)
10.9 1040 MPa HSFG, preloaded joints, automotive, compact flanges Hot-dip galvanizing required
12.9 1220 MPa Precision machinery, die tooling, aerospace sub-assemblies Outdoor, corrosive, or impact-load environments

When in doubt between grades, choose the lower grade and increase the number of bolts — this is almost always cheaper and more reliable than stepping up to a higher grade with smaller safety margins.

Step 4 — Choose Material by Environment

The bolt material determines its base corrosion resistance before any coating. There are three common families:

Carbon and alloy steel (grades 4.6 through 12.9)

The standard for structural and mechanical applications. Carbon steel has virtually no inherent corrosion resistance — it depends entirely on surface treatment (see Step 5). The advantage is the full range of strength grades, weldability, and cost efficiency. This is the right choice for any covered or coated environment.

Stainless steel A2 (ISO 3506 — 304 grade)

Good corrosion resistance in general outdoor, mildly acidic, and food-processing environments. Strength class A2-70 (equivalent to roughly 8.8 in tensile strength) is most common. Not suitable for prolonged chloride exposure — seawater, de-icing salt, or coastal splash zones.

Stainless steel A4 (ISO 3506 — 316 grade)

Molybdenum-alloyed for superior chloride resistance. Specify A4 for marine, coastal, chemical plant, and desalination applications. A4-80 provides the highest standard strength (800 MPa tensile). Available in hex bolts and structural fasteners from TERNS EXIM.

Stainless steel fasteners are not inherently stronger than carbon steel — A4-80 at 800 MPa matches grade 8.8. If you need both corrosion resistance and high strength (above 8.8 equivalent), consult us about duplex stainless or super-duplex options.

Step 5 — Select the Finish by Corrosion Exposure

For carbon steel fasteners, the surface finish determines corrosion life. The decision depends on exposure category and grade compatibility:

Finish Salt-Spray Hours Grade Compatible Typical Use
Plain (black) ~24 h All grades Covered/indoor, short-term protection
Zinc electroplated 72–120 h Up to 8.8 freely; 10.9 with bake General light outdoor, domestic
Hot-dip galvanized (HDG) 480–1000+ h 8.8 only (not 10.9/12.9) Structural outdoor, coastal construction
Geomet / Delta Tone 720–1000 h All grades including 10.9/12.9 Automotive, preloaded joints, wind energy
Mechanical zinc plating 200–500 h All grades High-strength bolts requiring zinc protection

Salt-spray hours are indicative. Actual performance depends on coating thickness and test method (ISO 9227).

Fastener Selection Checklist

Before placing an order, confirm you have specified all of the following:

  • Fastener type — hex bolt, socket cap screw, stud bolt, anchor bolt, etc.
  • Standard — ISO 4014/4017 (hex bolt), DIN 933, ASTM A193, or equivalent
  • Nominal diameter — e.g. M16 or 5/8 inch
  • Thread pitch — coarse (standard) or fine; metric or unified
  • Nominal length — grip length plus nut height plus washer thickness plus 2–3 thread pitch clearance
  • Property class / grade — 8.8, 10.9, 12.9, A2-70, A4-80, etc.
  • Material — carbon steel, alloy steel, stainless (specify grade)
  • Surface finish — plain, HDG, zinc electroplated, Geomet, etc.
  • Nuts and washers — matching grade and standard (ISO 4032 nut, ISO 7089 washer, etc.)
  • Certification — EN 10204 2.2 test report, or 3.1 MTC with heat number
  • Quantity — number of pieces; specify whether spares are included
