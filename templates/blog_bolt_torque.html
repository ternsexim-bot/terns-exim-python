How to Calculate Bolt Torque: Formula and Reference Table

Tightening a bolt to the correct torque is not about following a number from memory — it is about generating the right clamping force for the joint. The torque is just the input; the clamping force is the output. Everything between the two — friction, lubrication, coating, thread condition — affects how efficiently torque converts to tension. This article explains the formula, the variables, and how to apply them in practice.

Important: The torque values in this article are general reference figures for clean, newly manufactured fasteners under defined surface conditions. Always verify against the fastener manufacturer's datasheet, the relevant design standard, and your structural engineer's specification before use in any safety-critical or structural application.

The Torque-Tension Relationship

When you tighten a bolt, you are applying torque to stretch the bolt shank slightly — creating a clamping force that holds the joint faces together. This clamping force (the bolt preload) is what resists slip, vibration loosening, and joint separation in service.

The challenge is that torque and tension are not directly proportional. Approximately 90% of the applied torque is consumed by friction — roughly 50% at the thread faces and 40% under the bolt head or nut bearing face. Only about 10% goes into stretching the bolt to create preload. This means small changes in friction — from lubrication, coating, surface finish, or contamination — can cause large changes in the actual clamping force for a given torque value.

The Torque Formula: T = K × D × F

The standard simplified torque equation for metric fasteners is:

T = K × D × F
  • T — tightening torque (N·m)
  • K — nut factor / torque coefficient (dimensionless)
  • D — nominal bolt diameter (m) — e.g. M16 = 0.016 m
  • F — target bolt tension / clamp force (N)

The target tension F is typically set at 70–75% of the bolt's proof load. For example, for a grade 8.8 M16 bolt, the proof load (ISO 898-1) is approximately 78,400 N (for the 157 mm² stress area). At 70% utilisation, F = 54,900 N. With K = 0.20 (plain), T = 0.20 × 0.016 × 54,900 = 176 N·m.

What the K Factor Is — and How Coating Affects It

The K factor (nut factor) is an empirical constant that captures the total frictional effect in a given nut-bolt-surface combination. It cannot be derived purely from first principles for real assemblies — it is measured by torque-tension testing (ISO 16047).

Surface Condition Typical K Factor Notes
Plain steel, as-supplied (light mill oil) 0.18–0.22 Default for dry structural connections
Zinc electroplated, no lubricant 0.17–0.20 Slightly lower than plain due to zinc surface
Hot-dip galvanized + wax-lubricated nut 0.14–0.18 HDG nuts are typically supplied with wax; verify with supplier
Geomet / Delta Tone coating 0.12–0.17 Built-in friction modifier; check manufacturer datasheet
PTFE / dry-film lubricant 0.12–0.15 Consistent but low — risk of over-tensioning if using standard tables
Moly-disulfide (anti-seize) paste 0.13–0.16 Used for stainless to prevent galling; reduces torque required

The practical implication: if you are using a Geomet-coated 10.9 bolt (K ≈ 0.14) and apply a torque based on a dry-steel K = 0.20, you will generate approximately 40% more clamping force than intended — potentially exceeding the bolt's proof load and causing plastic deformation or fracture. Always match the K factor to the actual surface condition of the fasteners being installed.

Under-Torquing and Over-Torquing Risks

Under-torquing is the more common field problem. A joint with insufficient preload:

  • Cannot develop the friction-grip needed to resist shear by slip
  • Allows bolt loosening under vibration or dynamic loading
  • May open gap between joint faces under tensile load
  • Is particularly dangerous because it is invisible — the bolt looks tight from outside

Over-torquing is less visible and often more serious:

  • Stresses the bolt shank into yield or beyond — reducing its load capacity
  • Can fracture the bolt immediately (common with grade 12.9) or cause delayed fracture under service load
  • Damages the thread form, preventing reliable retightening
  • Is most dangerous with high-K lubricants applied without reducing torque target

The practical risk range around a torque specification is large. Studies have shown that installation by experienced trades using good-quality tools produces clamping forces within ±25% of target. Poor equipment or incorrect K factors can push this to ±50% or more. This is why safety factors are built into structural bolt calculations — and why they should not be eroded by using the wrong torque.

Using Calibrated Tools

Any bolted joint with a specified torque requires a calibrated torque wrench. Key points for tool management:

  • Accuracy class: EN ISO 6789 Class B torque wrenches must deliver ±4% of the indicated value. This is the minimum acceptable for structural work. Cheaper non-certified wrenches may be ±10% or worse.
  • Calibration interval: Recalibrate at least annually or after any overload event (e.g. dropping the wrench, using it as a breaker bar). Calibration must be traceable to a national standard (UKAS, DAkkS, NABL, or equivalent).
  • Operating range: Each wrench has an optimal operating range, typically 20–100% of full scale. Using a 500 N·m wrench to tighten an M8 bolt (24 N·m target) is imprecise — use the correct range wrench.
  • Pneumatic and electric tools: Impact guns are not torque-controlled. Use them only for run-down, not for final torquing. Electronic angle-and-torque tools (shut-off nutrunners) are acceptable for production environments with verified calibration.
